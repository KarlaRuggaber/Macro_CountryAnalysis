### Slide 3 – Denmark: GDP per Capita

We start with Denmark, covering the period from 1995 Q1 to 2024 Q4. Looking at real GDP per capita, Denmark shows a steady upward trend, growing at roughly 1% per year since 2000. This is consistent with a mature, high-income economy converging to its long-run growth path.

Two major disruptions are clearly visible. The Global Financial Crisis in 2008–09 caused a drop of around 7%, and recovery was slow — it took nearly five years to return to trend. The COVID shock in 2020 was sharper but shorter, with a drop of around 5% followed by a rapid rebound. Both episodes confirm that Denmark follows standard business cycle dynamics despite its welfare state buffers.

---

### Slide 4 – Denmark: Business Cycles

Applying the Hodrick-Prescott filter with lambda equal to 1600 to isolate the cyclical component, we see that Denmark's output gap is quite persistent — the autocorrelation is around 0.84, meaning a positive gap today predicts a positive gap next quarter. This is one of the higher persistence values across our three countries.

Unemployment and inflation follow expected patterns. Unemployment averages around 5% over the sample, with a notable spike to about 8% during the GFC. Inflation has averaged around 1.8% since 2000 — close to the ECB's target, which makes sense since Denmark maintains a fixed exchange rate peg to the euro through the ERM II mechanism.

---

### Slide 5 – Denmark: Stylised Facts

Comparing Denmark to peer economies on the cross-country GDP chart, we see it comfortably in the high-income cluster alongside Germany and the UK, well above South Korea and far above China and India — consistent with Kaldor's first stylised fact of sustained positive growth.

The factor shares chart confirms the remaining stylised facts hold reasonably well for Denmark. The capital share alpha is about 37%, implying a labour share of around 63%, and both are relatively stable over time. This is exactly what SF6 and SF7 predict — no systematic trend in factor shares. Denmark is therefore a textbook case for the Kaldor stylised facts.

---

### Slide 6 – Denmark: Solow Model

Calibrating the Solow model using Penn World Table data from 2000 to 2019, we get the following parameters. Alpha is 0.366, the savings rate is 26.3%, depreciation 4.1%, population growth 0.5% per year, and TFP growth 0.1% per year. These feed into a steady-state capital intensity of about 15.35 and output of 2.72 in effective units.

The key policy experiment is a savings shock. Since the savings rate s equals 26.3% and alpha is 36.6%, we have s less than alpha — Denmark is below the Golden Rule. This means increasing the savings rate moves the economy closer to the Golden Rule steady state and raises long-run consumption per worker. The transition path is smooth, reaching the new steady state over several decades, which illustrates the diminishing returns mechanism at the core of the Solow model.

### Slide 8 – Norway: GDP per Capita

Norway has the longest sample of our three countries, running from 1978 Q1 to 2024 Q4, and it has by far the highest GDP per capita — reflecting the oil wealth premium. Trend growth since 2000 is around 1% per year, similar to Denmark, but the level is substantially higher.

Several episodes are worth noting. The late 1980s show a trough associated with the domestic banking crisis of 1987 to 1989, caused by rapid credit expansion after financial deregulation. The 2015–16 oil price collapse caused only a mild dip, which demonstrates how the Government Pension Fund Global — the sovereign wealth fund — acts as a shock absorber. The COVID contraction was also relatively contained at around 3%, again buffered by fiscal capacity.

---

### Slide 9 – Norway: Business Cycles

Norway's output gap has an autocorrelation of about 0.60 — lower than Denmark's, reflecting the oil sector's idiosyncratic volatility. Crucially, Norway has the lowest output standard deviation of our three countries at 0.013, again consistent with the oil fund smoothing fiscal policy over the cycle.

Unemployment is strikingly low — averaging around 3.7% since 2000, the lowest of our three countries. This reflects both a tight labour market and the income effect of oil wealth. Inflation has averaged about 2.5% since 2000, slightly above the Norges Bank's 2% target, partly because oil revenues create domestic demand pressure.

---

### Slide 10 – Norway: Oil Anomalies

This is where Norway diverges most sharply from the standard model. Looking at factor shares, Norway is a notable exception to Kaldor's SF6 and SF7. The labour share swings wildly between 38% and 65% across the sample, moving in direct opposition to the oil price cycle. When oil prices are high, operating surplus in the petroleum sector surges, inflating the capital share. When oil prices fall, the labour share recovers. This is not a story about technology or capital accumulation — it is simply the oil price.

The cross-country comparison confirms Norway sits well above the peer group trajectory. This premium is not explained by faster TFP growth — it is almost entirely a level effect from resource extraction.

---

### Slide 11 – Norway: Solow Model

Norway's Solow calibration reveals two important anomalies. First, alpha is 0.505 — greater than 0.5 — entirely because oil rents inflate measured capital income. In a standard economy without resource extraction, the capital share is typically around 1/3. Second, and more unusual, TFP growth g is actually slightly negative at minus 0.6% per year over 2000–2019. This reflects the maturing of Norway's oil fields: as reserves deplete, total factor productivity in the economy-wide sense declines.

The policy experiment we simulate is reinvesting a larger share of oil revenues through the GPFG rather than spending them domestically. Channelling more oil wealth into the sovereign fund raises the effective national savings rate — s increases — and the model shows the economy transitioning to a higher domestic steady state. In the long run, this builds wealth that generates returns for future generations.

### Slide 13 – Sweden: GDP per Capita

Sweden's data runs from 1993 Q1 — starting right in the aftermath of the currency crisis when Sweden was forced to abandon its ERM peg in November 1992. Despite this challenging starting point, Sweden has been the fastest-growing of our three countries, with trend growth of approximately 1.25% per year since 2000.

Two recessions stand out. The GFC caused a drop of roughly 7%, similar to Denmark. But the COVID shock was actually the deepest of the three countries at around 8% — Sweden's more open, trade-exposed economy made it particularly vulnerable. Recovery in both cases was relatively swift, reflecting the flexibility built into Sweden's post-1992 reform framework.

---

### Slide 14 – Sweden: Business Cycles

Sweden has the highest output volatility of our three countries, with a standard deviation of 0.018. The output autocorrelation is 0.67, placing it between Denmark and Norway.

Unemployment tells a clear structural story. Starting near 10% in 1993 — the aftermath of the ERM crisis, bank failures, and recession — it declined steadily through the late 1990s and 2000s, settling around 6–7% before the GFC. There is a visible ratchet effect: each recession pushes unemployment up, and it never fully returns to its pre-crisis level. Inflation has averaged around 1.5% since 2000 — persistently below the Riksbank's 2% target — a chronic undershoot that has made Swedish monetary policy a topic of academic interest.

---

### Slide 15 – Sweden: Solow Model

Sweden's Solow parameters are broadly similar to Denmark's. Alpha is 0.384, the savings rate is 25.9%, depreciation 4.0%, population growth 0.8%, and TFP growth 0.4%. The steady-state capital intensity is 13.32 and effective output is 2.70.

Like Denmark, Sweden is below the Golden Rule — s equals 25.9% while alpha is 38.4%, so increasing savings raises long-run consumption. Sweden also shows the highest TFP growth rate of the three countries when we account for the oil distortion in Norway, suggesting stronger innovation and productivity dynamics. The Solow model fits Sweden well: smooth convergence to steady state with no structural anomalies.

*No text needed.*

### Slide 17 – Comparison: GDP per Capita & Growth

Putting all three countries together on a common sample from 1993, the contrast in levels is clear. Norway is consistently around 30–40% richer in per capita terms, driven entirely by oil. Denmark and Sweden track closely together in level terms, though Sweden's faster trend growth means it is gradually closing the gap with Denmark.

The growth rate comparison shows all three countries are highly synchronised at cyclical frequencies — they all dip together in 2008–09 and 2020 — which reflects their tight trade and financial integration within the Nordic region and the broader European economy. In the long run, however, Sweden's structurally higher growth rate compounds over time.

---

### Slide 18 – Comparison: Labour Market & Inflation

The unemployment comparison highlights the structural differences most starkly. Norway maintains the lowest unemployment throughout — the oil fund creates both fiscal space and income that supports full employment. Denmark sits in the middle. Sweden remains structurally higher, a legacy of the 1992–93 banking and currency crisis that reset the unemployment rate to a permanently higher level.

On inflation, the three countries broadly converge after 2000 as all three operate inflation-targeting frameworks. The 2021–23 energy and supply-chain inflation spike hit all three similarly, though Norway's oil exports meant it benefited from the terms-of-trade improvement even as domestic prices rose.

---

### Slide 19 – Comparison: Solow Model

The Solow comparison crystallises the key finding of the cross-country analysis. Norway is the outlier on almost every parameter: alpha above 0.5, negative TFP growth, and savings behaviour distorted by the oil fund mechanism. These are not signs of a more or less productive economy — they are signs of a resource economy where standard growth accounting breaks down.

Denmark and Sweden, by contrast, are broadly similar in their Solow structure. Both sit below the Golden Rule, both have positive TFP growth — Denmark around 0.1%, Sweden around 0.4% — and both show smooth convergence in the tilde variables. The main difference is Sweden's slightly higher alpha and higher population growth, giving a somewhat lower steady-state capital intensity. In all three cases, the policy implication is the same: saving more would increase long-run welfare, which is consistent with the Nordic model's emphasis on high institutional savings through pension systems and sovereign funds.

To summarise: all three countries are high-income, stable, and broadly confirm the Solow model's predictions — with Norway as the instructive exception that shows what happens when resource extraction dominates the national accounts.
